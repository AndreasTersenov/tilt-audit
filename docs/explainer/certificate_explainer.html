tilt-audit · a research story told slowly · 2026-07-04

Can a sampler tell you when it’s lying?

A patient walk through one research arc: why scientists steer generative models, why nobody can currently tell whether the answers are trustworthy, how we built a meter that was supposed to fix that, and exactly how — and why — it failed. Nothing under the carpet.

part 1 · the goal

What we are actually trying to do, and why it matters

Start with the science problem underneath everything. A telescope measures a distorted, noisy trace of the universe’s matter. From it, cosmologists want the matter map itself. But many different maps are compatible with the same noisy data — so the honest answer is not one map. It is a range of plausible maps, each with its probability. Statisticians call this the posterior distribution; you can think of it as the answer with its error bars built in. The same structure appears in medical imaging (which tissue images are consistent with this scan?) and in drug design (which molecules are consistent with these desired properties?).

To compute a posterior you need two ingredients. First, the data you measured. Second — and this is the subtle one — a notion of what maps are plausible in the first place, before seeing any data. That second ingredient is called the prior, and the modern move is to get it from machine learning: train a generative model (the same technology as AI image generators) on physics simulations, and it absorbs what realistic matter maps look like. This works remarkably well — these models capture texture and structure that older mathematical priors never could.

But a generative model, by itself, only produces typical maps. To answer a scientific question you must make it produce maps that are both plausible and consistent with your particular data — you must sample the posterior, not the prior. The methods that do this are called steering (or “guidance”), and here is the uncomfortable truth this whole project lives on:

Nearly every steering method in actual use is a mathematical shortcut with no guarantee. And on real data there is no way to check the output, because checking would require knowing the true posterior — the very thing you are trying to compute. Results from these pipelines go into scientific papers with error bars nobody has verified.

Our research program attacks this from a specific angle: build a simplified world where the true posterior IS known exactly, and use it as a test bench. In our world, the “universe” is a Gaussian random field — a kind of structured random texture — chosen because for this one family, sixty-year-old theory (Wiener filtering) gives the exact posterior in closed form. Not approximately: exactly, to machine precision, in any dimension. On this bench we can measure precisely how wrong each steering method is, and — the subject of this page — test instruments that might carry over to real data, where no bench exists.

Two nights of bench experiments preceded this story. They established that the popular shortcuts are measurably, substantially wrong at realistic settings — one workhorse method lands 5–28× farther from the truth than an ideal sampler would, its error growing with steering strength and problem size. So the danger is real, not hypothetical. The question became: can a running sampler be made to report its own error on real data? That instrument — a warning light for steered samplers — was the day’s goal.

part 2 · background

The three ideas you need

Idea 1 — A diffusion model generates by undoing noise, in many small steps

Take a real map and add a little random noise. Add a little more, and more, until nothing but static remains. That destructive direction is trivial. The clever part: a neural network is trained to run it backwards — given a somewhat-noisy map, estimate a slightly-less-noisy one. Generation then works by starting from pure static and applying this denoising network a few hundred times. Watch the structure of the process:

background · how a diffusion model generates

Idea 2 — Steering modifies each step; the correct target is written down by Bayes’ rule

An unsteered model walks back from static to a typical map. To get maps consistent with your data, steering methods add a small extra pull at every one of those hundreds of steps — a nudge toward agreement with the measurements. The strength and direction of that nudge is where all the approximation lives: the theoretically correct nudge involves a quantity the model doesn’t directly know (how uncertain its own denoising currently is), so practical recipes substitute something cheaper. The most popular one simply ignores that uncertainty. On our bench, that shortcut produces answer clouds that are too confident: centered slightly wrong, and far too narrow — precisely the failure mode that corrupts scientific error bars.

Meanwhile, what the steered sampler should produce is not mysterious. Bayes’ rule specifies it exactly, and it has a form worth internalizing because the meter is built on it: the correct output is the unsteered model’s own random walks, re-weighted at the end by how well each final map fits the data. In principle you could sample the posterior with no steering at all: generate thousands of typical maps, keep the ones that happen to match your measurements. That is hopeless in practice (essentially no typical map matches), which is why steering exists — but it defines, in checkable form, what “the right answer” means.

Idea 3 — Importance weights: comparing two sets of rules on the same journey

One more classical tool. Suppose a walker takes a journey following rules B, and you want to know how probable that same journey would have been under rules A. If both rules are known, you can compute, step by step, the ratio “probability of this move under A” over “probability of this move under B”, and multiply the ratios along the journey. Statisticians call the result an importance weight. It is an exact accounting device — no approximation — provided you genuinely know both rules.

Now the crucial observation: a steered sampler knows both rules about itself. It knows the unsteered model’s step rules (that’s just its own network without the nudge) and it knows the nudged rules (it applied them). So it can compute these weights about its own journeys, while it runs, on real data, with no ground truth anywhere. That is the loophole the meter exploits.

part 3 · the instrument

The meter, assembled from those three ideas

Here is the whole construction, honestly, in four steps:

  1. Run your steered sampler normally. It produces, say, 28 final maps, each via a few hundred nudged random steps. Nothing extra happens to the sampler.
  2. As each journey unfolds, keep a ledger. At every step, record the ratio: probability of the move just taken under the unsteered rules, versus under the nudged rules actually used. Multiply along the journey. This is the importance weight of Idea 3 — the “journey cost” of all the nudging.
  3. At the end, multiply in the data fit of the final map (how well it agrees with the measurements). By Idea 2, journey cost × data fit is exactly the weight that converts “what the steered sampler did” into “what Bayes says it should have done.”
  4. Read the ledger. A mathematical identity does the rest: if the sampler is producing exactly what it claims, every journey ends up with the same weight. Any spread in the weights is caused by — and quantifies — the gap between what the sampler did and what it should have done. Flat ledger = certified. Spread-out ledger = error, and the amount of spread is the reading.

Two numbers summarize the ledger. The effective sample count: if one journey carries almost all the weight, your 28 samples are really worth ~1 (spread has eaten them). And the meter reading: a single error score computed from the spread, which mathematics guarantees is an upper limit on how wrong the final answer cloud can be. (Its units, “nats,” are the standard currency of information — all you need here is: 0 means perfect, bigger means worse, and it adds up across independent sources of error.)

Run it a few times. With honest steering the ledger sits at the dashed line, the effective count stays near 28, and the meter reads near zero — correctly, as the bench-only “true error” readout confirms. With the shortcut, the endpoint cloud visibly overshoots and narrows (that’s the overconfidence), a handful of journeys grab all the weight, and the reading jumps. On the full-size bench experiments this instrument priced a good sampler’s error to within 5% — at every problem size we tried, up to maps of 16,384 components. So far, a real instrument.

part 4 · first crack

The meter measures the journey. Science cares about the destination.

Look once more at step 2 of the construction: the ledger accrues a cost for every nudge at every step. Now imagine a sampler that pulls too hard early on, drifts off course, and then partly recovers — landing not far from the right place. Its destination error is modest. But its ledger remembers the whole detour: the journey error is enormous. The mathematical guarantee still holds — journey error is always an upper limit on destination error — but the limit can be absurdly loose. part 5 · the failure

Trained networks turn every journey into a detour

Everything above used mathematically perfect ingredients — the bench’s exact formulas standing in for the neural network. The whole point, though, is to deploy on trained networks. So we swapped in real ones (trained on our bench universe, so the truth stays checkable), and the instrument drowned. Here is the mechanism, which the previous paragraph already set up.

The steering nudge is computed through the network’s own gradients — roughly, “in which direction should this noisy map change to fit the data better, according to the network?” A trained network answers that question jaggedly: its gradient carries substantial rapidly-fluctuating error on top of the true signal. The fluctuations largely cancel over hundreds of steps — the sampler still lands in a reasonable place — but every one of those fluctuations was a nudge the ledger dutifully billed. The journeys become permanent detours. In our runs the sampler’s own emergency brake (a standard clamp that limits any single step’s nudge) was firing on up to 70% of all steps — a direct, independent witness of how violent the ride is. On the real trained networks the measurements were: meter readings 8–125× larger than with exact gradients at identical steering; effective sample counts pinned at 1–3 out of 256; and the reading dominated by the network’s gradient noise rather than by the steering error it was designed to isolate. The meter had become a gradient-roughness detector. That is a perfectly honest thing to measure — it is just not the thing anyone needs.

The rescue attempts — each tried, each measured

Component-wise ledgers. Our bench universe separates cleanly into thousands of independent components, and a ledger can be kept per component — smaller books, less compounding. On perfect ingredients this worked spectacularly (component ledgers reproduced the exact answer to four digits). On trained networks: effective count ~3 of 256 per component. The gradient noise contaminates every component at every step; the books are ruined locally, not just globally.

Gentler steering. Practitioners rarely run the nudge at full theoretical strength; maybe the meter works in the regime people actually use? We ran the ladder — quarter strength, half, full: the ledger stays degenerate at every setting. Fewer steps — a shorter journey to bill? Worse (each step’s nudge gets bigger). More samples — average the noise away? We went to 65,536 journeys; no change, because the spread grows much faster than averaging can repair.

The fairest question: rankings. Even a biased meter is useful if it can compare — “on my network, is recipe A better than recipe B?” Comparisons even cancel the one quantity the meter can’t know (a normalization constant), making them its statistically cleanest output. So we built four variants of the same recipe at different steering strengths, asked the meter to rank them, and checked against the bench’s exact truth:

part 6 · due diligence

Auditing our own verdict

A negative result is worth publishing only if it is airtight, so we attacked it before believing it:

  • NO BUGWe rebuilt the entire computation in independent closed-form mathematics and compared against the running code — through the exact same code path a trained network uses, with a deliberately corrupted model so any bookkeeping error would surface. Agreement: one part in 100,000.
  • MECHANISM CONFIRMEDThe failure follows quantitatively from one measured fact — trained-network gradients are jagged — and disappears exactly when smooth gradients are substituted, at every steering strength.
  • NO ESCAPE FOUNDComponent-wise books, gentler steering, shorter runs, more samples, rankings instead of absolute readings: each tested, each measured, none survives.

Every stage of this arc was pre-registered: before each experiment we wrote down what we expected, with a confidence percentage, and afterwards scored ourselves publicly — including the misses, of which this day produced several. The one-line conclusion, now measured rather than suspected:

On trained networks, how much a sampler travels tells you nothing about where it lands — and any instrument built on journey-accounting inherits that blindness.

part 7 · what remains

Why this was a good day anyway

Three things survive the wreck. First, the meter remains genuinely useful on the bench, where its tightness for good samplers is a precision tool for method developers. Second, the post-mortem is a map: it says the next instrument class must examine the destination — the final samples themselves — rather than the journey; that is a concrete, different mathematical starting point, with its own literature to check before anyone gets excited. Third, and maybe most transferable: one exact test bench plus pre-registered predictions killed a plausible, seductive research program in a single day, for the cost of a few GPU-hours — before months went into building it. Most fields cannot do that. Ours, for this one question, now can.
