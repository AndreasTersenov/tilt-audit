tilt-audit · a one-day research story · 2026-07-04

Can a sampler tell you when it’s lying?

We built a runtime warning light for steered generative models, tested it against exact mathematical truth, and killed it — then attacked our own verdict until we were sure. This page explains what the meter was, how it could read anything at all without knowing the right answer, and precisely why it fails where it was needed most.

scene 1 · the problem

Steering without a speedometer

Modern science increasingly uses generative diffusion models as priors: train one on simulated maps of the universe (or molecules, or MRI images) and it learns what plausible objects look like. To answer a real question you then steer the generator — “give me plausible maps, but only ones consistent with my telescope’s data.” Nearly every steering recipe in use is a mathematical shortcut, and on real data nobody can check the result, because on real data nobody knows the true answer. There is no warning light. That gap is what we set out to fill.

scene 2 · the arena

A universe where the answer is known

Our lab is a deliberately simplified universe — a Gaussian random field — where sixty-year-old theory gives the exactly correct answer to every steering problem, in closed form. Methods can be graded there with no wiggle room. Two nights of experiments in this arena had already shown that popular steering shortcuts are badly biased, and had even caught real bugs in the community’s own validation tools. The next step was the warning light itself: an instrument that could ride along with any steered sampler, on real data, and report how far it is from the answer it claims to produce.

scene 3 · the instrument

Self-accounting: how a meter can read without truth

The trick is that the meter never compares against the true answer. It audits the sampler’s own books. Every steering method is a modified version of the plain, unsteered generator, and at each of its ~hundreds of small steps the method knows exactly what modification it applied. So, along each generated trajectory, we accumulate two ledger entries:

Journey cost — how improbable each step it took would have been under the unmodified generator’s rules; and destination fit — how well the final sample actually agrees with the data. Exact mathematics (importance weighting on path space) says: combine the two into one number per sample, and if the sampler truly produces what it claims, all samples end up with equal weights. A distorted sampler produces wildly unequal weights. Flat books = honest. Spiky books = trouble.

With the honest sampler, the weights sit level and the meter’s reading agrees with the true error it cannot see — on the full-scale experiments it priced a good sampler’s error to within 5% at every size we tested. With the biased shortcut, the books go spiky: a few trajectories carry all the weight, the effective sample count collapses, and the reading jumps by orders of magnitude. Tight when green, loud when red — on perfect ingredients, a real instrument.

scene 4 · the fine print

Loud — but honest about the wrong thing

One asymmetry showed up immediately in the exact bookkeeping: for a biased sampler the meter over-states the damage — by a factor of nearly 100. The reason matters later. The meter measures the whole journey; the scientist cares about the destination. A biased sampler tends to over-pull early and partially relax back, so its path strays far more than its final answer does. Real numbers from the arena (16×16 problem, one steering strength):

scene 5 · the failure

Trained networks: the journey stops predicting the arrival

Then we swapped the perfect ingredients for the real thing — a trained neural network score, the pilot night’s own checkpoints. The instrument drowned. Steering a real network means pulling on its learned gradients, and those gradients are violent: the sampler thrashes so hard along the way that its built-in safety clamp fired on up to 70% of steps — yet it still lands in a reasonable place, because the thrashing largely averages out. The meter, which reads the journey, screams regardless of the landing. Watch the same instrument with network-like noise injected into the steering force:

On the full-scale experiments the numbers were brutal: the trained network’s readings came out 8–125× larger than the exact-score pathway’s at identical steering, per-sample effective counts pinned at 1–3 out of 256, and no rescue anywhere — not gentler steering (practitioners’ regime), not shorter runs (worse), not more samples (65,536 changed nothing).

scene 6 · the tombstone

The fairest test it could get — and the verdict

One hope remained. Even a meter that can’t give absolute numbers could still be useful if it ranks: “on my network, steering recipe A is better than B.” So we built a family of variants — the same recipe at four steering strengths — and asked the meter to pick the best, with the arena knowing the true answer. scene 7 · the audit of the audit

Making sure the kill was real

Before writing the obituary we attacked our own conclusion, three ways:

  • VERIFIEDThe meter’s code, run through the exact same pathway a trained network uses, agrees with independent closed-form mathematics to one part in 100,000. Not a bug.
  • NO RESCUEGentler, practitioner-style steering strengths (0.1–0.5×): the books stay degenerate at every setting; shorter runs are worse.
  • NO RESCUEThe fairest use case — ranking similar guided variants against each other — is where it anti-correlates with the truth (scene 6).

The obstruction is fundamental to this instrument class, and now it is measured, not suspected: on trained networks, how much a sampler travels does not tell you where it lands.

epilogue

What survives, and why this was a good day

The meter remains a genuine instrument inside the arena, where exact references exist — a method-developer’s tool, tight for good samplers and loud for bad ones. The deployment problem — a warning light for real data — remains open, and the post-mortem points the way: the next instrument class must read the destination, not the journey. Every step of this arc was pre-registered — predictions with confidences written down before each run, then scored, misses included. One day, one plausible flagship idea, built, killed, and verified killed, with the reasons in closed form. That is what the arena is for.
